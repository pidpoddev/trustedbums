# Trusted Bums UX Optimization Backlog

_Last updated: 2026-06-04 by Codex daily UX consultant automation._

## Executive Read

The highest-confidence UX issues are still concentrated in trust-critical intake and recovery flows. Public signup still clears a manually entered company name when the email no longer matches a known client workspace, and the homepage contact form still relies on destructive toasts instead of field-level recovery guidance.

The new terms-and-agreements work shipped real progress, but the client dashboard still sends users to `Company Profile` instead of the dedicated agreement flow and still discards `deniedFrom` context from client-role redirects. That leaves access boundaries and required next steps feeling arbitrary at the point where users most need reassurance.

Authenticated live validation remains blocked from this runner. After sourcing `.env.qa`, `pnpm run qa:env` passed, but `curl -I -L --max-time 20 https://trustedbums.com` still timed out during DNS resolution and `pnpm exec playwright test tests/e2e/authenticated-role-smoke.spec.ts --project=chromium --reporter=line` still failed all 5 checks on the first `page.goto("/")` with `net::ERR_ABORTED`. Today’s recommendations are therefore code-backed and source-backed, not freshly browser-verified.

## Active Recommendations

### P1 - Preserve typed company names in the public signup flow
- Evidence: [src/components/SignupIntentDialog.tsx:44](src/components/SignupIntentDialog.tsx:44) still sets `companyName` to `""` whenever a client email no longer matches `getKnownClientForEmail(email)`, so editing the email can erase a manually entered company name before signup is complete. Current Baymard guidance still treats clearing entered data during form recovery as a high-friction failure that harms conversion ([Baymard](https://baymard.com/learn/checkout-flow-ux-optimization)).
- Why it matters: This is a first-touch trust step. Silent data loss makes the platform feel brittle before a prospect has any reason to trust the workflow.
- Recommendation: Preserve manually entered `companyName` unless the user explicitly accepts a matched workspace. Keep matched-workspace autofill, but separate it from first-time workspace entry so email edits do not wipe prospect input.
- Acceptance criteria: Editing the email no longer clears a manually entered company name when no match exists; matched workspaces still prefill clearly; first-time workspace requests keep typed company data through recovery.

### P1 - Add field-level validation and reassurance to the homepage contact form
- Evidence: [src/pages/Index.tsx:144](src/pages/Index.tsx:144) still validates only `name`, `email`, and `message` inside `submitContactForm` and reports failures through destructive toasts. The rendered form in [src/pages/Index.tsx:544](src/pages/Index.tsx:544) still lacks inline error copy, linked summary behavior, and `aria-invalid` wiring. Current W3C and GOV.UK guidance still recommends text error identification, linked summaries, and accessible invalid-state wiring rather than generic toast-only recovery ([W3C SC 3.3.1](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/error-identification), [GOV.UK Error summary](https://design-system.service.gov.uk/components/error-summary/)).
- Why it matters: This is the public conversion handoff for buyers and prospective Bums. Toast-only validation forces users to hunt for what failed and weakens confidence at the exact point where they are deciding whether to share contact details.
- Recommendation: Add inline validation for each invalid field, a linked error summary on submit, `aria-invalid` and described-by wiring, and a short trust cue near the submit button that sets reply expectations.
- Acceptance criteria: Invalid submit shows field-level messages and a linked summary; invalid fields expose accessible error state; recovery does not require guessing which field failed; the submit area explains expected follow-up timing or handling.

### P1 - Make dashboard recovery paths match access and agreement states
- Evidence: [src/components/ClientAccessRoute.tsx:29](src/components/ClientAccessRoute.tsx:29) still redirects unauthorized client users to `/client/dashboard` with `state={{ deniedFrom: location.pathname }}`, but [src/pages/client/ClientDashboard.tsx](src/pages/client/ClientDashboard.tsx) still does not read or surface `deniedFrom`. The same dashboard still routes outstanding agreement actions to `/client/profile` at [src/pages/client/ClientDashboard.tsx:115](src/pages/client/ClientDashboard.tsx:115) and [src/pages/client/ClientDashboard.tsx:130](src/pages/client/ClientDashboard.tsx:130), even though the dedicated agreement surfaces are [src/pages/client/ClientAgreements.tsx:32](src/pages/client/ClientAgreements.tsx:32) and [src/pages/client/ClientTerms.tsx:139](src/pages/client/ClientTerms.tsx:139). Current W3C notification guidance still recommends telling users what happened and what to do next ([W3C notifications](https://www.w3.org/WAI/tutorials/forms/notifications/)).
- Why it matters: Silent redirects and misleading next actions make role boundaries and legal-state requirements feel arbitrary, which raises support load and reduces trust in the client portal IA.
- Recommendation: Teach the dashboard to display a dismissible recovery notice when `deniedFrom` is present, name the current client role, point to the nearest allowed workflow, and send agreement actions to `/client/agreements` or `/client/terms` instead of `Company Profile`.
- Acceptance criteria: Redirected users see a visible notice naming the blocked destination, their current role, and a next allowed action; agreement prompts link to the real agreement flow; ordinary dashboard visits show no recovery banner.

### P1 - Add urgency and next-step cues to the admin handoff queues
- Evidence: [src/pages/admin/AdminHandoffs.tsx:255](src/pages/admin/AdminHandoffs.tsx:255) still sorts target responses, intro requests, and contact conversions by age, but the queue tables at [src/pages/admin/AdminHandoffs.tsx:355](src/pages/admin/AdminHandoffs.tsx:355) only expose status, owner, created timestamp, and update actions. The backing models already include `admin_next_action`, `admin_priority`, and `notification_error` in [src/lib/contactApi.ts:33](src/lib/contactApi.ts:33) and [src/lib/portalApi.ts:737](src/lib/portalApi.ts:737), but those cues are not surfaced in the queue. Current USWDS table guidance still recommends brief, scannable cells for dense operational tables ([USWDS tables](https://designsystem.digital.gov/components/table/)).
- Why it matters: This page is positioned as the admin rescue workspace. Without urgency and next-step context, operators must infer what is stalled, which slows lead follow-up, Bum response triage, and intro-request recovery.
- Recommendation: Surface SLA aging state, next-action text, priority state, and notification-failure state directly in each queue row, plus a compact queue summary that distinguishes fresh, stale, and unowned work.
- Acceptance criteria: Items beyond the chosen SLA threshold are visually distinct; admins can see the next required action without drilling into another page; queue summaries distinguish fresh vs stale work; contact submissions with notification failures are visibly marked for manual follow-up.

### P2 - Reduce commission-plan request friction to a minimum viable schedule
- Evidence: [src/pages/client/ClientOpportunityNew.tsx:1214](src/pages/client/ClientOpportunityNew.tsx:1214) through [src/pages/client/ClientOpportunityNew.tsx:1269](src/pages/client/ClientOpportunityNew.tsx:1269) still mark Year 2 through Year 6+ commission inputs `required`, even though the request payload accepts blank later-year values and normalizes them upstream. Current W3C form guidance still recommends asking only for what is truly required and clearly distinguishing optional detail from mandatory input ([W3C Forms](https://www.w3.org/WAI/tutorials/forms/), [W3C validation](https://www.w3.org/WAI/tutorials/forms/validation/)).
- Why it matters: Clients often know the near-term commission structure before they know the full long-tail schedule. Requiring six percentages up front increases hesitation and encourages invented data during opportunity setup.
- Recommendation: Make Year 1 the only default required rate, move later-year percentages behind an optional multi-year schedule choice, and explain how omitted later years are handled during review.
- Acceptance criteria: A client can submit a commission-plan request with a minimum viable required set; later-year fields are clearly optional unless the user opts into a multi-year schedule; helper text explains how omitted years are treated.
